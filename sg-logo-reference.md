# Logo & Lettermark Design Reference — Saint Germain Physical Therapy

## Part 1: What Makes a Good Logo (General Principles)

### Simplicity
- [ ] Recognizable at a glance — works in under 2 seconds of viewing
- [ ] No more than 2–3 core visual elements
- [ ] Avoid gradients or effects that don't survive print/fax/embroidery
- [ ] Works as a single-color silhouette (the "fax test")
- [ ] A child could roughly sketch it from memory (the "napkin test")

### Scalability
- [ ] Legible at 16px favicon size
- [ ] Still striking at billboard scale
- [ ] No fine details that collapse at small sizes
- [ ] Maintains proportions without distortion at any aspect ratio crop

### Memorability
- [ ] Contains a distinctive visual hook — one thing that makes it "stick"
- [ ] Avoids generic clip-art associations
- [ ] Unique silhouette compared to competitors
- [ ] Passes the "describe it to a friend" test — can be verbally summarized

### Versatility
- [ ] Works on light backgrounds, dark backgrounds, and photography
- [ ] Effective in full color, single color, and reversed (white-on-dark)
- [ ] Adapts to horizontal, stacked, and icon-only lockups
- [ ] Suitable for digital (screen), print (CMYK), and physical (signage, embroidery, engraving)

### Timelessness
- [ ] Avoids trendy effects (current-year gradients, excessive skeuomorphism)
- [ ] Would not look dated in 10–15 years
- [ ] Draws from enduring typographic and geometric traditions
- [ ] Classic ≠ boring — timelessness comes from proportion and restraint, not plainness

### Appropriateness
- [ ] Tone matches the brand's personality and audience
- [ ] Does not inadvertently resemble another well-known mark
- [ ] Culturally neutral — no unintended meanings across audiences
- [ ] Typographic weight matches the brand's voice (authoritative vs. approachable)

## Part 2: What Makes a Good Lettermark

### Legibility of Initials
- [ ] Both letters (S and G) are immediately identifiable
- [ ] Letters don't get "lost" inside each other — each has its own visual space
- [ ] Readable without needing the brand name spelled out alongside it
- [ ] Even when stylized, the underlying letter anatomy is respected

### Interplay & Composition
- [ ] Letters have a deliberate spatial relationship (overlapping, nested, mirrored, interlocking)
- [ ] Negative space is used intentionally — either as breathing room or as a hidden element
- [ ] Visual weight is balanced — one letter doesn't overpower the other
- [ ] The composition has a clear "center of gravity" and doesn't feel lopsided

### Kerning & Spacing
- [ ] Optical kerning, not mechanical — spacing looks even to the eye, not the ruler
- [ ] The S and G pair is tricky: both have open bowls facing the same direction; good kerning accounts for this
- [ ] Letter spacing contributes to the mark's personality: tight = modern/bold, loose = elegant/airy
- [ ] Tracking is consistent with the wordmark if used together

### Monogram Techniques Worth Exploring
- **Ligature**: Letters share a stroke (e.g., the spine of S becomes the crossbar of G)
- **Nesting**: One letter sits inside the other (e.g., S tucked into the open bowl of G)
- **Stacking**: Letters arranged vertically with shared visual axis
- **Mirroring/Rotation**: Rotational or reflective symmetry between letters
- **Containment**: Letters placed inside a geometric frame (circle, shield, rounded square)
- **Overlap with transparency**: Semi-transparent overlap revealing a third color/tone

## Part 3: Logo Design for Physical Therapy Practices Specifically

### Industry Expectations & Conventions
- [ ] Conveys trust, professionalism, and clinical competence
- [ ] Feels welcoming, not intimidating — patients are often in pain or anxious
- [ ] Avoids looking like a hospital/ER (not too clinical or sterile)
- [ ] Avoids looking like a gym/CrossFit box (not too aggressive or "bro")
- [ ] Sits in the sweet spot: medical credibility + human warmth

### Color Psychology for PT
- **Blues**: Trust, calm, clinical authority — the safe default
- **Greens**: Healing, renewal, nature, growth — very common in PT
- **Teals/Aquas**: Modern freshness, combines trust (blue) with growth (green)
- **Warm neutrals (taupe, cream, sage)**: Boutique, upscale, spa-adjacent
- **Deep navy or charcoal + gold/copper accent**: Premium, established, serious
- **Avoid**: Aggressive reds, neon colors, black-heavy palettes, overly playful pastels

### Typography for PT Practices
- **Serif fonts**: Convey tradition, trust, and establishment — good for "Saint Germain" which has an old-world elegance
- **Humanist sans-serifs**: Approachable yet professional (e.g., Gill Sans, Frutiger family)
- **Geometric sans-serifs**: Clean and modern but can feel cold — use with warm colors
- **Avoid**: Script/handwriting fonts (hard to read on signage), ultra-condensed fonts, novelty fonts
- **"Saint Germain" specifically**: The name has French heritage and elegance — typography should honor that without being pretentious

### Motifs & Symbolism in PT Logos
- [ ] **Movement/motion**: Implied through curves, arcs, forward lean
- [ ] **The human body**: Abstract figure, spine, joint articulation
- [ ] **Hands**: Healing hands, support, manual therapy
- [ ] **Upward trajectory**: Recovery, progress, getting back on your feet
- [ ] **Circles/arcs**: Wholeness, range of motion, continuity of care
- [ ] **Negative space anatomy**: Spine or figure revealed in the lettermark itself

### What Distinguishes a Premium PT Practice Logo
- [ ] Restraint — fewer visual elements, more white space
- [ ] Typography does the heavy lifting, not illustration
- [ ] Color palette limited to 2–3 colors maximum
- [ ] The mark works as a standalone icon (for app icons, embroidery, window signage)
- [ ] Name treatment feels like it belongs to a boutique, not a chain
- [ ] "Saint Germain" should feel like a proper noun, not a label

### Common Pitfalls in PT Logos
- [ ] Generic stick figure in motion (overused, forgettable)
- [ ] Spine illustration that looks like every chiropractor's logo
- [ ] Too many colors or gradients
- [ ] Clip-art-adjacent imagery
- [ ] Typography that prioritizes "Physical Therapy" over the practice name
- [ ] Logos that look like they came from a template site

## Part 4: Evaluation Rubric for the S+G Lettermark Candidates

Rate each candidate 1–5 on these dimensions:

| Criterion | Weight | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **Legibility** | High | Can you read S and G immediately? |
| **Distinctiveness** | High | Would you remember this tomorrow? |
| **Elegance** | Medium | Does it honor the "Saint Germain" name? |
| **Versatility** | Medium | Works at all sizes, on all backgrounds? |
| **Movement** | Low | Does it subtly suggest motion or healing? |
| **Scalability** | Medium | Clean at 32px and 320px? |
| **Warmth** | Medium | Approachable to a patient in pain? |
| **Professionalism** | High | Would a referring physician take this seriously? |
