Design Reference & Criteria

General Logo Principles

  • Recognizable at a glance — works in under 2 seconds
  • No more than 2–3 core visual elements
  • Works as single-color silhouette (the "fax test")
  • Legible at 16px favicon and at billboard scale
  • Contains a distinctive visual hook that sticks
  • Effective in full color, single color, and reversed
  • Would not look dated in 10–15 years
  • Could be sketched from memory on a napkin

Lettermark Essentials

  • Both S and G immediately identifiable
  • Letters don't get lost inside each other
  • Optical kerning — spacing looks even to the eye
  • S+G pair: both have open bowls facing same direction — kerning must account for this
  • Visual weight balanced — neither letter overpowers
  • Composition has a clear center of gravity
  • Techniques: ligature, nesting, stacking, mirroring, containment, overlap

Physical Therapy Specifics

  • Conveys trust and clinical competence
  • Welcoming, not intimidating — patients are often anxious
  • Sweet spot: medical credibility + human warmth
  • Avoid looking like hospital/ER (too clinical)
  • Avoid looking like gym/CrossFit (too aggressive)
  • Implied movement through curves, arcs, forward lean
  • Name hierarchy: "Saint Germain" dominant, "Physical Therapy" secondary

Color Psychology for PT

  • Blues: Trust, calm, clinical authority
  • Greens: Healing, renewal, growth
  • Teals: Modern freshness, trust + growth
  • Warm neutrals: Boutique, upscale, spa-adjacent
  • Navy + gold: Premium, established, serious
  • Avoid: Aggressive reds, neons, black-heavy palettes

Typography Guidance

  • Serifs: Tradition, trust — good for "Saint Germain"
  • Humanist sans: Approachable yet professional
  • Geometric sans: Clean, modern — pair with warm colors
  • "Saint Germain" has French heritage — honor elegance without pretension
  • Avoid: scripts, ultra-condensed, novelty fonts
  • Tight kerning = modern/bold; loose = elegant/airy

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Generic stick figure in motion (overused)
  • Spine illustration = every chiropractor's logo
  • Too many colors or gradients
  • Clip-art-adjacent imagery
  • Prioritizing "Physical Therapy" over practice name
  • Logos that look like they came from a template site
